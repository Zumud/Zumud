Everyone agrees resumes should be tailored per job; the question is how
to do it without spending an hour per application. The realistic options
are doing it by hand, using a keyword scanner to guide manual edits, or
letting an AI tool rewrite the resume for you. Each trades time against
control differently.

## What are the options?

| Approach | Examples | Time per application | What you get |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Fully manual | You + a text editor | 20–60 min | Full control, best judgment, does not scale |
| Keyword scanner + manual edits | Jobscan | 10–20 min | A match report; you still do the rewriting |
| AI resume builder | Teal, Rezi, Kickresume | 5–15 min | Editor-centric platforms with AI-assisted rewriting |
| AI tailoring generator | Zumud | About 1 min | A tailored, ATS-friendly document generated from your resume + the job post |

## When is manual tailoring the right call?

When the application genuinely warrants an hour: a dream role, a referral
on the line, a senior position where every line will be probed in
interviews. Manual work gives you complete control over framing and
guarantees nothing is misstated. Its weakness is consistency — most
people tailor carefully for the first three applications, then start
sending the master resume as fatigue sets in, precisely when volume
matters. The method itself is straightforward; we lay it out in
[how to tailor your resume to a job description](/guides/how-to-tailor-your-resume-to-a-job-description).

## What do keyword scanners actually do?

Tools like Jobscan compare your resume against a pasted job description
and report a match score: which hard skills, titles, and terms the
posting contains that your resume lacks. That diagnosis is genuinely
useful — it automates the tedious extraction step. But the scanner does
not write anything; you still rewrite bullets and reorder content
yourself. Think of it as a checklist generator that cuts manual
tailoring time roughly in half.

## What about AI resume builders?

Platforms like Teal, Rezi, and Kickresume center on an online resume
editor with AI assistance: rewrite this bullet, generate a summary,
score the resume against a job. They suit people who want to build and
manage their resume inside one platform, often alongside job-tracking
features. Two trade-offs to check on any builder: whether your data and
document stay exportable (avoid platforms that lock your resume into
their hosted format), and whether the AI invents content — always review
line by line that everything stated is true.

## How is Zumud's approach different?

[Zumud](https://zumud.com) is a generator, not an editor: you upload the
resume you already have, paste the job post, and it produces a tailored
version in about 30 seconds. The differences that matter:

- **Output is LaTeX-built.** You get an ATS-safe, single-column PDF plus
  the .tex source, editable in Overleaf — no platform lock-in. (Why that
  helps parsing: [LaTeX resumes](/guides/latex-resume-guide).)
- **It tailors, it does not fabricate.** The rewrite reworks emphasis,
  keywords, and ordering from your real experience.
- **It is open source**, and the first 10 generations are free, then
  EUR 0.10 each — pay-as-you-go rather than a subscription.

The honest limitation: Zumud is not a resume-management platform. If you
want a dashboard to track fifty applications and edit in-browser
continuously, a builder like Teal fits that shape better. If you want
each application to go out with a properly tailored, cleanly parsing
document in the least time, a generator is the right tool.

## So which should I use?

Match the tool to your application volume. A handful of high-stakes
applications: manual, possibly with a scanner's keyword report. Steady
applying — several per week — where consistency collapses first: an AI
tool, reviewed by you before sending. The one approach that reliably
underperforms is the one most people default to: sending the same
untailored resume everywhere.
