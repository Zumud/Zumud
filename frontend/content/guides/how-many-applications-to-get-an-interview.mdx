There is no single true number, but the published research converges on
uncomfortable odds: a corporate job posting attracts on the order of 250
applications, and only a handful of those people are interviewed. The
useful question is not the exact ratio — it is which factors move it,
because they are largely within your control.

## What do the published numbers say?

The most-cited figures come from Glassdoor and recruiting-industry
research: a corporate job opening attracts about [250 resumes on
average](https://drjohnsullivan.com/articles/why-you-cant-get-a-job-recruiting-explained-by-the-numbers/),
of which 4 to 6 people are called to interview and 1 is hired.
That is roughly a 2% application-to-interview rate for a cold applicant
— which matches the common job-seeker experience that landing one
interview takes dozens of untargeted applications.

Two more research findings frame why the funnel is so tight:

- Harvard Business School's Hidden Workers study found [over 90% of
  employers use applicant tracking or recruiting-management software](https://www.hbs.edu/managing-the-future-of-work/Documents/research/hiddenworkers09032021.pdf)
  to filter and rank applicants before a human reads them — and that
  these filters exclude many viable candidates on technicalities.
- Ladders' eye-tracking research measured the recruiter's initial
  resume scan at [7.4 seconds](https://www.theladders.com/career-advice/you-only-get-7-4-seconds-to-make-an-impression-how-recruiters-see-your-resume).
  Even when you pass the software, the human decision is nearly
  instant.

## What actually moves the ratio?

The 2% baseline describes an *untargeted* application: one generic
resume mass-sent to postings. Each of these factors changes the odds:

| Factor | Why it moves the needle |
| --- | --- |
| Tailoring the resume per posting | Screening software ranks you by match to that posting's language; the recruiter's 7-second scan looks for the same match |
| Applying early | Applications reviewed before a shortlist forms face less competition |
| Genuine qualification fit | No edit rescues an application for a role two levels away |
| Referrals | Skip most of the screening funnel entirely |
| Clean, parseable formatting | A resume the software cannot read never ranks at all |

The first and last rows are pure execution — every applicant can do
them. The method for the first is in
[how to tailor your resume](/guides/how-to-tailor-your-resume-to-a-job-description);
the formatting rules are in
[what makes a resume ATS-friendly](/guides/what-makes-a-resume-ats-friendly).

## What is a realistic plan built on these numbers?

Plan for volume, but refuse to let quality drop with it. Concretely:

1. **Set expectations from the baseline.** At cold-application rates,
   an interview per 30–50 applications is normal, not a verdict on you.
2. **Tailor every application** — this is where most applicants leave
   their edge on the table, because manual tailoring takes 20–60
   minutes and fatigue wins. This is the step worth automating:
   [Zumud](https://zumud.com) tailors your resume to a pasted job post
   in about 30 seconds, output as an ATS-safe LaTeX PDF (first 10
   generations free).
3. **Track your own funnel.** If 40+ tailored applications to roles you
   match produce zero interviews, the resume itself needs a rework —
   run the copy-paste parse test and rewrite your bullets around
   outcomes.
4. **Spend saved time on the channels with better odds**: referrals
   and direct outreach, which bypass the 250-resume pile.

## The bottom line

Expect dozens of applications per interview at baseline; that is what
the research describes. But the baseline assumes the average applicant's
behavior — an untailored resume, judged by software in milliseconds and
by a human in 7 seconds. Tailored content and clean formatting are the
two levers fully in your control, and they act on both judges at once.
